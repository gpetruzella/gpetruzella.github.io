Course Description:

What does it mean to be an American?

 

This semester we will read and write about American identities. We will question entrenched beliefs, common sense, and explore the origins of American ideas and ideals. You will be asked to critically read, analyze, and write about films, podcasts, and canonical essays on the American condition. All graded assignments build on one another, and include a variety of formal and informal writing exercises.

Process Goals

If you fully engage with the resources presented in this course, you will leave with the following:

  • An understanding that good college-level writing involves a process of revising and editing across multiple drafts.
  • The ability to both give and draw from constructive feedback during the revision process.
  • The ability to critically and carefully read a diversity of texts written for different audiences and purposes.

 

Writing Goals

If you fully engage with the resources presented in this course, you will leave with the following:

  • The ability to respond to a variety of rhetorical situations to effectively reach different audiences.
  • The ability to use different genres (or types of writing) to reach different audiences.
  • The ability to use library databases to find peer-reviewed academic sources.
  • The ability to evaluate, synthesize, critique, and add your own ideas to published sources.
  • The ability to effectively and ethically summarize, paraphrase, quote, and cite published sources to achieve your purposes.
  • The ability to vary your texts’ structure, paragraphing, tone, style, and grammar depending on genre and audience.
  • The ability to edit and proofread your writing.

Plagiarism

In my experience, most instances of plagiarism are not intentional but are the result of a student not understanding what qualifies as plagiarism, or not understanding that all material directly taken from a text, or even paraphrased text, must be properly cited. Please consult your Little Seagull Handbook, section R-4 “Integrating Sources and Avoiding Plagiarism” (pp. 107-118) for useful tips on how to avoid plagiarism. Please see the “Academic Honesty, Plagiarism, and Fraud” heading under the “Course Policies” section of this syllabus for my strict plagiarism policy (page 3). A-F Grade Distribution for Formal Written Assignments

You may be used to receiving a letter grade on every assignment that you turn in; however, in this course, you will only receive a letter grade on your final draft. This is because revision, editing, and improvement based on peer and instructor feedback are all part of the writing process. You will be graded on your engagement with this entire writing process. Therefore, you will only receive a grade on your final draft; however, you will receive comprehensive feedback throughout your entire writing process.

 

A-F Grading Scale

Though specific criteria will vary depending on assignment (and you’ll be given a prompt with genre expectations for each assignment), in general, your letter grades correspond to the following descriptions:

 

A, A- =EXCELLENT:

 

A-level writing shows sophistication and creativity in relation to the assignment’s rhetorical situation; has a clear focus throughout; is well-structured, with clear transitions that show the text’s logic, or “flow”; shows sophisticated use of source material as evidence, including proper source integration and citation practices (if part of the assignment); shows a balance between ideas drawn from outside sources (if applicable) and the writer’s own; and last, but not least, shows stylistic and grammatical fluency in relation to the audience it is meant to address. In sum, “A” papers show mastery of the assignment’s rhetorical situation, leaving the reader with new ideas and a lasting final impression.

 

B+, B, B- = GOOD:

 

 B-level writing shows the writer’s ability to respond to an assignment’s rhetorical situation in a clear, adequate, if not entirely original way; shows aptitude in organizing and connecting ideas to the essay’s overall focus, though occasionally there might be brief lapses in “flow” between ideas; shows adequate use and citation of source material as evidence (when required), though integration between source material and writer’s ideas may at times be awkward; and, though clearly written to a specific audience, may show occasional errors, wordiness, or vague phrasing. Overall, “B” papers clearly and competently address the assignment and show an adequate engagement with the rhetorical situation.

 

C+, C, C- = ACCEPTABLE:

 

C-level writing responds to the rhetorical situation in a basic way; it may present an overly simple argument or an argument that is implicit rather than explicit; it may appear mostly organized, but could use more consistency with topic, transition, and conclusion sentences; it may rely on summary, general analysis or repetitive content rather than in-depth analysis of source material to forward new ideas; clear distinction between evidence from source material and writer’s own ideas may be absent; style may be generic (in 5-paragraph form, for instance), showing little attention to audience, and there may be multiple errors. In sum, a C paper fulfills the major parts of the assignment, but there is room for improvement.

 

D = INADEQUATE:

 

 D-level writing inadequately addresses the assignment’s rhetorical situation; it may lack a clear focus, which affects its organization; examples may be disjointed or not parallel to the argument; when applicable, it may present some misunderstanding of source material, or lack a clear distinction between evidence from source material and writer’s own ideas; and it may contain a variety of errors throughout the paper that obscure meaning. Overall, D-level writing may complete the assignment, but the reader may struggle when trying to understand the writer’s intention in relation to the rhetorical situation.

 

F = UNACCEPTABLE:

 

 F-level writing does not meet the basic requirements of the assignment. It may show a misunderstanding of the assignment’s rhetorical situation in topic, genre, use of source materials, or page length; it may lack a clear focus or paragraphs; parts of the essay may be plagiarized and there may be a variety of errors that obscure meaning for the reader. Overall, F-level writing suggests that there is misunderstanding of the assignment and class discussions pertaining to the assignment’s rhetorical situation, or that more time should be spent drafting and revising the paper, with feedback from me, your peers, and/or Writing Associates.

Hector St. John Crevecoeaur’s Letter 3 in his text Letters from an American Farmer (1783). We will write about how these early Americans conceptualized a distinct American identity separate from Colonial or British identity. You will write a reflection about what being American or American identity means to you.
