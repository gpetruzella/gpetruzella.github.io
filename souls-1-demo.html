About This Site

William Edward Burghardt Du Bois was born in 1868 in Great Barrington, Massachusetts, to a free-black family. He attended the local schools and excelled in his studies, eventually graduating as valedictorian of his class. However, when in 1885 he began attending Fisk University in Tennessee, he encountered for the first time the open bigotry and repression of the Jim Crow South, and the experience had a profound impact on his thinking. Du Bois returned to the North to further his education, with nothing less than equal rights for black Americans being his goal. When he earned his Ph.D. from Harvard University in 1895, he was the first black man to have done so, and his dissertation, "The Suppression of the African Slave Trade to the United States of America, 1638–1870," was one of the first academic works on the subject.

This website is a transcription of his most famous work, The Souls of Black Folk that was published in 1903. In it, he recounts the problems of race in American culture, which he defined as the problem of the twentieth century. He writes about African-American cultural, political and social relevance to American history and civic life and documents how black people were central figures in the formation of the country and how and why the country failed to continue support for civil rights for blacks in the aftermath of Reconstruction.

I. Section of Chapter 1

Du Bois used the first stanza of James Russell Lowell’s poem "The Present Crisis" to headline the second chapter of The Souls of Black Folk. Lowell, a New England poet wrote the poem in 1845 about that national slavery crisis. This works well with this chapter because it stresses the aftermath of the Civil War and "The Present Crisis" of the newly freed. Although sympathetic to the abolitionist cause because of his first wife Maria White, Lowell was ambivalent about African Americans. Nonetheless, his poem has remained important in African American life and culture. Later, Du Bois chose the title of the poem for the NAACP journal. Martin Luther King, Jr. often quoted this first stanza in many of his speeches.
